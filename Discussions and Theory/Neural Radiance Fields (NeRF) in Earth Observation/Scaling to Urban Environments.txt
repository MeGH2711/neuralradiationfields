Scaling standard Neural Radiance Fields (NeRFs) to large, city-scale environments is computationally very challenging. These models often struggle to capture the large geometric complexity and fine details of entire cities present within a single network, because of the GPU memory limits and very long training duration.

To overcome these limitations, the researchers have utilized a divide-and-conquer chunking strategy to process large aerial datasets more efficiently. Frameworks like Block-NeRF and Mega-NeRF scale volume rendering to large environments by dividing the scenes into smaller geographical blocks, which can be processed independently \cite{cite8}. Instead of depending on a single massive network, each of the individual sub-regions is assigned its own NeRF model, which is trained separately \cite{cite8, cite7}.

This approach effectively reduces the computational load by distributing, which allows the model to be more optimized in parallel while bypassing the memory bottleneck that is typically seen in large-scale 3D modelling. During the inference, the appropriate network is chosen and rendered based on the location of the camera within the partitioned grid. By distribution of the scene representation across multiple networks these chunking methods enables scalable modeling of large city environments, allowing continuous virtual fly-throughs and realistic novel view synthesis across entire urban areas.
