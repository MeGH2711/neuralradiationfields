In multi-date photogrammetry, image datasets are often collected over a long period of time, capturing the same scene at different times of the year. This long time span introduces notable appearance differences, especially in the case of seasonal changes such as the vegetation shifting from green to brown in autumn, or the presence of snow during winter.

Earlier Earth observation frameworks, such as S-NeRF and Sat-NeRF, use explicit solar angle modelling to reduce illumination differences, but often struggle in handling seasonal changes. Solar angles are weakly related to the seasonal variations, so the models that rely only on solar position cannot completely correct the pixel consistencies that are caused by the seasonal variations.

To overcome this limitation, methods like Season-NeRF introduce time as an explicit variable in the neural rendering framework. By including this temporal information, the model learns to capture season-specific features. This design allows the network to combine time-based appearance representation along with varying solar angles. This enables it to stimulate how geographic regions change across different seasons \cite{cite5}.
