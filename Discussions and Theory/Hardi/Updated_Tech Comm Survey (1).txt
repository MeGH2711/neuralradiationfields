Challenge: Remote sensing datasets, particularly those that involve multi-view satellite constellations or multiday UAV missions, are basically multi-temporal \cite{cite5}. Basically, images are obtained over vast time scales, leading to major changes in image appearance caused by changes in sun angle, atmospheric conditions, and phenology  \cite{cite5, cite9}, EOGS++ Earth Observation Gaussian Splatting with Internal Camera Refinement and Direct Panchromatic Rendering]. These radiometric changes cause major noise in the neural supervision signal, challenging the multi-view consistency assumption essential for stable optimization \cite{cite5, cite8}. This means that typical models tend to suffer from convergence instabilities where the model is unable to detect surface reflectance from lighting conditions or atmospheric changes \cite{cite5, cite9}.

RQ: How do these neural models maintain geometric integrity when the underlying supervision signal shows global and local radiometric changes due to seasonal or atmospheric changes? \cite{cite5}

PS: To maintain geometric and radiometric consistency, neural frameworks make a distinction between static scene geometry from temporary appearance and lighting dynamics. Appearance-adaptive latent is employed to describe the style of acquisition, with SatGS using a hierarchical model in which global embeddings at the pre-image level describe broad atmospheric changes, while local embeddings at the pre-Gaussian level accommodate spectral fine-tuning \cite{cite5}.

To improve modelling of acquisition-specific sensor responses, for example, EOGS++ uses learnable affine transformations for each camera, which map the invariant 3D features to colour channels at a specified timestamp \cite{cite9}. Instead of treating shadows as noise, S-NeRF and SatGS treat shadows as important information for geometry \cite{cite5, cite11}. This utilizes solar visibility fields and surrounding light from the sky to ensure consistency of shadows and buildings \cite{cite11, cite5}. Furthermore, in order to improve the robustness of the approach against temporary objects like moving vehicles, UPNeRF utilizes the uncertainty and visibility fields. This results in variance maps that reduce the loss for unreliable pixels, which prevents degradation of permanent scene structures \cite{cite13}.

Challenge: Remote sensing data is primarily captured from top-down (nadir). This results in “Top-Down Bias”, where vertical structures like building front-view suffer from significant information loss and structural ambiguity. This large difference in perspective makes the establishment of geometric correspondences between aerial rooftops and facades on the ground level an inherently ill-posed problem \cite{cite14}. Without the availability of sufficient oblique imagery to provide side-view context, neural models often have difficulty disentangling the conflated foreground buildings from the background terrain, leading to “jagged” or “sawtooth” effects or a complete absence of the vertical elements.

RQ: How do neural rendering frameworks solve “Top-Down Bias” inherent in vertical remote sensing imagery to accurately reconstruct vertical building surfaces and eliminate geometric features like jagged edges or the merged surface with ground?

PS: Neural frameworks leverage explicit geometric control of 3D Gaussian Splatting (3DGS) and specialized loss functions to recover high-fidelity vertical structures. To address the “Top-Down Bias” limitation in vertical reconstruction, these modern frameworks combine oblique and multi-perspective imagery for providing the necessary side-view context that is missing in predominantly nadir datasets \cite{cite15, cite16}. For example, pipelines like Gaussian Building Mesh (GBM) and Ortho-3DGS utilize the benefits of multiple angular views to recover high-fidelity vertical geometry that is hidden or deformed in standard satellite-based reconstruction \cite{cite17,cite2}. This is further improved in cross-view tasks through the use of pseudo-aerial views at a significant pitch angle, like 45° or 60°, that allow the model to penetrate foliage and capture building sides shared by both ground and aerial views \cite{cite14}. To complement this data integration, PG-SAG uses semantic-aware scene partitioning to address structural uncertainty between foreground buildings and surrounding background \cite{cite8}. By using subgroups for scene partitioning, the model ensures that vertical facades do not overlap with the terrain \cite{cite8}. PG-SAG introduces a boundary-aware normal loss to eliminate “jagged” or “sawtooth” effects commonly seen in traditional methods. This loss term reformulates the optimization process along the detected edges in a way that reduces normal vector ambiguity, which ensures better structural preservation \cite{cite8}. Lastly, GBM-like frameworks provide better vertical fidelity by using morphological operations and/or contour simplification on building masks. This makes sure that the building bases are well-defined, which reduces any errors resulting from having no building masks fully cover the ground and the vertical facades of the building.
