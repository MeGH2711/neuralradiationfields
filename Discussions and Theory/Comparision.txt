The main difference between 3DGS and NeRF lies in their computational requirements. NeRF represents a scene using a Multilayer Perceptron (MLP), which requires the entire network to be queried a number of times for sample points along each camera ray during volume rendering. This highly dense sampling creates and heavy computational overhead, which often results in several hours of training time, and rendering speed that takes seconds or minutes per frame \cite{cite6}. For example, on a large-scale urban reconstruction, NeRF required more than 16 hours of training on a single GPU \cite{cite10}.

In contradiction, 3DGS uses an explicit scene representation, which is made up of 3D Gaussian ellipsoids. These ellipsoids are directly projected onto the 2D image plane using a highly parallelized, tile-based rasterizer, which prevents the unnecessary calculation in empty space \cite{cite6,cite2}. This explicit representation greatly reduces the training time and also enables real-time rendering at speeds of over 100 frames per second (FPS) \cite{cite1,cite5}.

Both NeRF and 3DGS models exhibit exceptional capabilities in capturing complex view-dependent effects and lighting. However, 3DGS outperforms NeRF in quantitative image quality metrics—such as PSNR, SSIM, and LPIPS—particularly in large-scale urban environments \cite{cite10}.

3DGS outperforms in preserving high-frequency structural features effectively, generating sharper boundaries around building edges, rooftops, and fine surface textures \cite{cite8}. On the other hand, NeRF models often struggle to represent high-frequency scene features, which may lead to blurred edges, visual artifacts, or overly smooth structures \cite{cite18,cite5}.

The traditional photogrammetry often fails in the areas that do not have any texture, but both of these models (i.e., 3DGS and NeRF) have the capability to recover continuous geometry even in uniform regions such as water bodies or plain walls \cite{cite3,cite2,cite6}. However, a well-known limitation of the standard 3DGS is that it can suffer from geometry distortion and excessive Gaussian expansion when only sparse views are available for learning. To achieve competitive accuracy comparable to that of the NeRF models in such cases, 3DGS often requires additional depth supervision or few-shot regularization techniques \cite{cite1,cite2}.

The underlying architecture of these two models affects their post-processing flexibility. NeRF models often behave like a ``black-box", where the features from the scenes are embedded into implicit weights of the MLP. As a result, segmenting and isolating specific objects, editing the scene, or removing noise becomes difficult. Furthermore, combining the large chunks that are split into separate MLP chunks can cause inconsistencies at the boundaries \cite{cite2}.

3DGS provides much greater flexibility for editing and controlling the scene, due to its explicit point-based representation \cite{cite2}. Individual 3D Gaussians can easily be modified, removed, or classified using spatial bounding boxes or semantic masks \cite{cite1,cite8}.
