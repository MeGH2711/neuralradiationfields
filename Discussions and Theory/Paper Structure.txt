1. Introduction

1.1 Background and Motivation: The evolution of 3D reconstruction in Earth observation, moving from traditional Structure-from-Motion (SfM) and Multi-View Stereo (MVS) to deep learning and neural rendering
1.2 Scope and Organization: The objectives of the survey and a roadmap of the paper.

2. Fundamentals of Neural Rendering

2.1 Neural Radiance Fields (NeRF): Core mechanics, continuous 5D coordinate inputs, Multilayer Perceptrons (MLPs), and differentiable volume rendering
2.2 3D Gaussian Splatting (3DGS): Explicit scene representation using 3D Gaussian primitives (position, covariance, opacity, spherical harmonics) and alpha-blending rasterization pipelines

4. Neural Radiance Fields (NeRF) in Earth Observation

4.1 Illumination and Shadow Modeling: Frameworks like S-NeRF and Sat-NeRF that incorporate solar direction to explicitly model direct sunlight, ambient sky light, and transient objects
4.2 Temporal and Seasonal Dynamics: Techniques like Season-NeRF that introduce time variables to render seasonally specific features across multi-date collections
4.3 True Digital Orthophoto Map (TDOM) Generation: Frameworks like Ortho-NeRF that utilize orthogonal ray sampling (shooting rays vertically upward) rather than central projection to eliminate geometric distortions and jagged edges
4.4 Scaling to Urban Environments: Chunking strategies (e.g., Block-NeRF, Mega-NeRF) for handling massive city-scale datasets

5. 3D Gaussian Splatting (3DGS) in Earth Observation

5.1 Adapting 3DGS for Satellite Imagery: Frameworks like EOGS and EOGS++ that replace pinhole models with affine approximations, perform internal bundle adjustment via optical flow, and natively render panchromatic data without external pansharpening
5.2 Appearance-Adaptive and Shadow 3DGS: Methods like SatGS that utilize hierarchical feature embeddings (global and local) and solar angle inputs to adjust Gaussian colors for seasonal changes and shadow shifts
5.3 Mitigation of Sparse View Degradation: Techniques integrating monocular depth supervision, few-shot Gaussian Splatting (FSGS), and proximity-guided unpooling (e.g., HyGS-TDOM) to constrain geometry when image overlap is poor
5.4 Gaussian-Based Orthophoto and Mesh Generation: Ortho-3DGS and PG-SAG frameworks that utilize orthogonal projection matrices, semantic-aware grouping, and Truncated Signed Distance Functions (TSDF) for high-fidelity TDOMs and building meshes

6. Comparative Analysis: NeRF vs. 3DGS

6.1 Computational Efficiency: Training and rendering time comparisons (hours/days for NeRF vs. minutes for 3DGS; real-time FPS rendering for 3DGS)
6.2 Reconstruction Quality: Analysis of geometric fidelity, preservation of high-frequency details (like building edges), and handling of textureless areas
6.3 Editability and Explicit Control: The advantages of explicit Gaussian representations for scene editing, noise removal (filtering "floaters"), and mesh extraction compared to the "black box" nature of implicit MLPs

7. Open Challenges and Future Directions

7.1 Multi-Modal Integration: The potential for combining optical imagery with LiDAR, SAR, or utilizing multi-spectral satellite data more effectively
7.2 Improved Handling of Transient Elements: Further refining uncertainty modeling to completely filter out moving vehicles, changing vegetation, and other dynamic scene elements
7.3 Integration with Geographic Information Systems (GIS): Bridging the gap between computer vision coordinate systems and true high-precision georeferencing for practical mapping applications

9. Conclusion
Summary of the current state-of-the-art and the transformative potential of explicit Gaussian representations in superseding NeRF for satellite remote sensing and digital twin creation
