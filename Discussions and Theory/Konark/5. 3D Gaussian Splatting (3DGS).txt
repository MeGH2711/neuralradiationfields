5. 3D Gaussian Splatting (3DGS) in Earth Observation

5.1 Adapting 3DGS for Satellite Imagery: 
The adaptation of 3D Gaussian Splatting (3DGS) to the satellite domain requires resolving the limitations of the standard pinhole camera model, which is insufficient for the Rational Polynomial Coefficient (RPC) camera models typical of satellite sensors[11,13]. The EOGS (Earth Observation Gaussian Splatting) framework handles this by replacing resource-heavy RPC projections with approximate affine camera models, mapping 3D points to 2D image coordinates quickly [13]. Guided by this, EOGS++ removes the need for extra preprocessing by combining internal bundle adjustment (BA) directly into the training pipeline[13]. This internal BA leverages optical flow estimation to align rendered and reference images, correcting geolocation errors that would otherwise degrade reconstruction quality[13]. Additionally, EOGS++ introduces the "3-PAN" strategy, which reproduces high-resolution single-channel panchromatic (PAN) data into a three-channel representation[13]. This allows the model to directly visualize high-resolution data without needing any external pansharpening of lower-resolution multispectral imagery, which commonly introduces noise[13].

5.2 Appearance-Adaptive and Shadow 3DGS 
Satellite datasets are identified by extreme appearance variations due to their wide temporal span, displaying as seasonal vegetation shifts and dynamic shadows[11]. The SatGS framework addresses these challenges through a hierarchical strategy for appearance-adaptive adjustment [11]. It uses global feature embeddings for each training image to capture large-scale seasonal color shifts, supported by local embeddings for each Gaussian to optimize spatial-specific variations like differential snowmelt[11]. To handle changes in lighting conditions, SatGS incorporates solar angle inputs into a specific multilayer perceptron (MLP) to predict shadow colors[11]. These shadow features are layered on top of seasonal colors to accurately model the shape and hue of shadowed regions[11]. Additionally, SatGS combines uncertainty modeling using features from pre-trained extractors like DINOv2 to identify and ignore temporary objects such as moving cars, ensuring they do not introduce noise into the geometric reconstruction[11].

5.3 Mitigation of Sparse View Degradation
A difficult challenge for 3DGS in remote sensing is the weakening of geometry in sparse-view scenarios, which normally occurs at the boundaries of flight strips[6]. The Few-Shot Gaussian Splatting (FSGS) method reduces this by introducing a proximity-guided unsampling strategy for completion, allowing new ellipsoids to grow in low-sampled regions[6]. FSGS also implements monocular depth supervision from pseudo-novel views, applying geometric consistency even when multi-view overlap is as low as 2–5 views[6]. The HyGS-TDOM framework uses a hybrid approach, partitioning scenes based on image overlap[6]. It applies standard 3DGS to dense central regions while using the FSGS pipeline for sparse boundary areas[6]. This region-specific optimization allows for highly accurate reconstruction across the entire scene, significantly outperforming standard 3DGS in sparse conditions[6].

5.4 Gaussian-Based Orthophoto and Mesh Generation 
Generating high-fidelity True Digital Orthophoto Maps (TDOMs) and building meshes requires accurate geometric control and parallel processing for large-scale environments[6,15]. The Ortho-3DGS framework improves 3D building reconstruction by replacing the standard perspective projection with a flat parallel view, which helps in removing building facade irregularities during view synthesis[7]. To further refine geometry, Ortho-3DGS uses depth regulation via pre-trained models like DepthAnything to limit the placement of Gaussian ellipsoids[7]. For large-scale urban reconstruction, the PG-SAG pipeline introduces semantic-aware clustering[15]. It employs models like Language Segment Anything (LSA) to segment building masks and then groups these regions into subgroups for parallel optimization[15]. Final surface models are normally generated by visualizing depth maps from these optimized Gaussians and applying Truncated Signed Distance Function (TSDF) fusion to produce smooth, colored building meshes[5]. These advancements allow for rapid, automated 3D modeling of complex urban environments from a simple set of coordinates or building names[5].
